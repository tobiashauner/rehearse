# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Job seekers preparing for interviews, in two overlapping modes: anxious, time-pressured
users cramming the night before a real interview, and deliberate long-term practicers
building interview skill as a habit over weeks or months. The same person moves between
both modes depending on how close their next real interview is — the product needs to
serve a calm weekly practice session and a stressed last-minute cram equally well, not
pick one persona and optimize for it.

The job to be done: get a realistic mock interview grounded in their own resume, target
job description, and company context, receive structured feedback, and walk away feeling
more prepared and confident than when they started.

## Product Purpose

Interview Workspace helps users prepare for real job interviews through AI-generated,
evolving mock interviews — grounded in their uploaded resume, job description, and
company research rather than a fixed question bank. It gives structured, specific
feedback on every answer and tracks improvement across sessions, so preparing for an
interview feels like working with a coach, not taking a test.

## Brand Personality

Calm, focused, competent. Closer to a good coaching/wellness app (Headspace) than a
pure-efficiency power tool (Superhuman/Linear) — reassuring and warm, with real breathing
room in the layout, but still purposeful and a little serious, never fluffy. Confidence is
communicated through clarity and restraint, not cheerfulness.

## Anti-references

Must not feel like cold, clinical testing/assessment software — no sterile exam-room
feel, no proctor-like chrome, no stark pass/fail framing. The product should feel like a
supportive presence preparing someone for something that matters to them, not a system
grading them.

## Design Principles

- **Coaching, not testing.** Every screen should read as support, not evaluation — even
  when the content (a score, a gap, a missed answer) is critical.
- **Reassure without trivializing.** Calm and warm, but the stakes are real; avoid
  gamification (streaks, mascots, confetti, badges) — a real interview isn't a game.
- **Flex between calm practice and high-stress cramming.** The same interface serves a
  relaxed weekly session and a stressed night-before-the-interview session; don't design
  for only one of those emotional states.
- **Clarity over density.** Every screen answers "what should I do next," per the
  product spec's own UI principles — favor whitespace and hierarchy over packed
  dashboards or data-dense tables.
- **Visible progress over time.** Since the product is used repeatedly across weeks or
  months, improvement needs to be legible at a glance, not buried in a table.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline (color contrast, full keyboard navigation, screen reader support)
plus deliberate calm-under-stress considerations: respect `prefers-reduced-motion`
everywhere, avoid alarming/urgent-red treatments for low scores or missed answers, and
keep error and failure states non-punishing in tone given users are often already
anxious.
